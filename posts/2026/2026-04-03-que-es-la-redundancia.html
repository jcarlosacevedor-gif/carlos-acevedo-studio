¿Qué es la redundancia?

April 03, 2026 · Studio Notes

A sentence is considered redundant when it uses words that express the same idea twice. In English, there are word combinations that are always redundant, even if they may initially sound normal. Some examples include:

  • **advance forward**
  • **return back**
  • **repeat again**

Why are these expressions incorrect? The reason is simple:

  • "advance" already means "to move forward."
  • "return" already implies "to go back."
  • "repeat" indicates "to say again."

In my recent lessons, I've noticed there are subtler redundancies that can confuse students. For example:

  • "the time when," which can be expressed simply as "when."
  • "the place where," which can be shortened to "where."

Let’s look at some examples in context:

  • "That was when it happened." is clearer than "That was the time when it happened."
  • "This is where I left him." is more concise than "This is the place where I left him."

Eliminating redundancy helps you to:

  • Sound more natural in English.
  • Write more clearly and professionally.
  • Improve your performance on exams like TOEFL or IELTS.
  • Communicate with greater precision and elegance.

In other words, it’s about saying less but communicating better. The quality of good English is measured not only by correctness but by efficiency; every word should serve a purpose.

So, the next time you speak or write, ask yourself: **Am I repeating something without realizing it?** This simple question can elevate your communication skills to new heights.

  • "the time when", que puede expresarse simplemente como "when".
  • "the place where", que se puede resumir en "where".

  • "That was when it happened." es más claro que "That was the time when it happened."
  • "This is where I left him." resulta más conciso que "This is the place where I left him."
